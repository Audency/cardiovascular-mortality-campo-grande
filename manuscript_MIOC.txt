Temporal trends and epidemiological profile of cardiovascular disease mortality in Campo Grande, Mato Grosso do Sul, Brazil, 2013-2022

ABSTRACT

BACKGROUND Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) remain the leading cause of death worldwide. In Brazil, regional disparities in CVD mortality persist, yet epidemiological data from the Central-West region are scarce.

OBJECTIVES To describe the epidemiological profile and temporal trends of cardiovascular mortality in Campo Grande, Mato Grosso do Sul, from 2013 to 2022.

METHODS An ecological, descriptive, retrospective study was conducted using data from the Mortality Information System (SIM). Deaths were classified into five groups: ischaemic heart disease (IHD), heart failure (HF), arrhythmias (ARR), cardiomyopathies (CMP), and inflammatory heart diseases (INF). Crude and age-standardised mortality rates were calculated. Temporal trends were assessed using Poisson regression, Prais-Winsten regression, and joinpoint analysis.

FINDINGS A total of 9,791 cardiac deaths were recorded. IHD accounted for 77.4% of cases. An annual percentage change (APC) of +3.3% (95% CI: 2.5-4.0) was observed. Males had 59% higher mortality (IRR = 1.59; 95% CI: 1.47-1.72). A joinpoint at 2019 marked acceleration in mortality, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic.

MAIN CONCLUSIONS Cardiovascular mortality in Campo Grande showed a significant increasing trend over the decade, driven primarily by ischaemic heart disease, with marked sex and age disparities.

Key words: cardiovascular diseases; mortality; epidemiology; ecological study; Brazil


INTRODUCTION

Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) are the leading cause of death globally, responsible for an estimated 19.1 million deaths in 2020, accounting for approximately 32% of all deaths worldwide (Roth et al. 2020). In low- and middle-income countries, the burden is disproportionately high, and Latin America faces a growing epidemic driven by the epidemiological transition (Lopez-Jaramillo et al. 2014, Rubinstein et al. 2025).

In Brazil, CVD has consistently ranked as the primary cause of mortality for decades, with approximately 400,000 deaths per year attributed to diseases of the circulatory system (Oliveira et al. 2022). National studies have documented declining trends in age-standardised CVD mortality between 1990 and 2017, attributed to improvements in risk factor control, expanded primary healthcare, and advances in emergency care (Souza et al. 2016, Lotufo et al. 2021). However, this overall decline masks important regional heterogeneities: the Central-West and North regions have shown persistently higher or stagnating rates compared with the South and Southeast (Mansur & Favarato 2016, Brant et al. 2025).

Within the CVD spectrum, ischaemic heart disease (IHD) — particularly acute myocardial infarction (AMI) — constitutes the single largest contributor to cardiovascular mortality in Brazil (Santo et al. 2024, Silva et al. 2024). Heart failure (HF) mortality, by contrast, has shown a declining national trend that has been attributed to therapeutic advances in pharmacological management (Gaui et al. 2021, Stevens et al. 2022). Cardiac arrhythmias, cardiomyopathies, and inflammatory heart diseases, though less prevalent as underlying causes, contribute meaningfully to the overall burden and have been less thoroughly studied at the subnational level (Oliveira et al. 2024).

Sociodemographic disparities in CVD mortality are well-documented in Brazil. Males exhibit consistently higher mortality rates from IHD (Mansur & Favarato 2011, Santos et al. 2022), while lower educational attainment and lower socioeconomic status are associated with premature cardiovascular death (Brant et al. 2021, Cardoso et al. 2021, Duncan et al. 2016). Racial and ethnic disparities have also been reported, with Black and mixed-race populations experiencing higher cardiovascular mortality in some regions (Lotufo & Bensenor 2024). Furthermore, the place of death — whether in-hospital, at home, or in public spaces — serves as an indirect marker of access to emergency cardiovascular care, with a high proportion of out-of-hospital AMI deaths suggesting delays in seeking or accessing treatment (Brant et al. 2020a).

The COVID-19 pandemic profoundly affected cardiovascular mortality patterns in Brazil. Studies have documented excess cardiovascular deaths during 2020-2021, attributed to reduced hospital admissions, delayed presentations, and direct effects of SARS-CoV-2 infection on the cardiovascular system (Brant et al. 2020b, Normando et al. 2021, Alencar et al. 2022, Almeida et al. 2025). In Brazilian state capitals, excess cardiovascular mortality during the pandemic reached 15-20% above pre-pandemic levels (Santos et al. 2023).

Campo Grande, the capital of Mato Grosso do Sul state, had an estimated population of 898,100 inhabitants in 2022 and is the largest city in the Central-West border region. Despite its demographic and epidemiological significance, no comprehensive analysis of cardiovascular mortality trends has been conducted for this municipality. Understanding local patterns is essential for guiding public health policy and resource allocation.

The objective of this study was to characterise the epidemiological profile and analyse the temporal trends of mortality from cardiovascular diseases in Campo Grande, Mato Grosso do Sul, from 2013 to 2022, stratified by disease group, sex, age, and sociodemographic variables.


MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study design and setting — This was an ecological, descriptive, retrospective study encompassing the municipality of Campo Grande, capital of Mato Grosso do Sul state, located in the Central-West region of Brazil. Campo Grande covers an area of 8,118.4 km² and is administratively divided into 74 neighbourhoods. The study population comprised all residents of Campo Grande whose deaths were registered between 2013 and 2022. The ten-year period was selected because 2013 marked the beginning of stable data availability in the SIM database, and 2022 was the most recent year with complete data at the time the study was initiated.

Data sources — Mortality data were obtained from the Mortality Information System (SIM) of the Brazilian Ministry of Health. The SIM is a national vital registration system that has achieved adequate coverage (>95%) and acceptable quality for epidemiological analysis in state capitals since the early 2000s (Almeida et al. 2025). Population estimates were obtained from the Brazilian Institute of Geography and Statistics (IBGE), using intercensal estimates for 2013-2021 and the 2022 Census for the final year.

Case selection — All deaths among Campo Grande residents with an underlying cause of death coded as a cardiac disease according to the International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision (ICD-10) were included. Deaths were classified into five mutually exclusive pathophysiological groups: (i) ischaemic heart disease (IHD: I20-I25); (ii) heart failure and other cardiac conditions (HF: I50-I52); (iii) cardiac arrhythmias and conduction disorders (ARR: I44-I49); (iv) cardiomyopathies (CMP: I42-I43); and (v) inflammatory heart diseases, including pericarditis, endocarditis, and myocarditis (INF: I30-I41). Valvular heart disease cases (I05-I09, I34-I37) were initially considered but excluded from the analysis because no cases were identified as underlying cause of death.

Variables — The following variables were extracted from the SIM: year of death, sex, age (in years), race/ethnicity (White, Mixed/Pardo, Black, Asian, Indigenous), marital status, educational level (illiterate, primary, secondary, higher education), place of death (hospital, home, other healthcare facility, public road, other), and underlying cause of death (ICD-10 code). Age was categorised into four groups for epidemiological analysis: 0-19 years (children/adolescents), 20-39 years (young adults), 40-59 years (adults), and ≥60 years (elderly). For age-standardisation, nine age groups were used following the WHO standard population (Segi-Doll modified).

Statistical analysis — Descriptive statistics were used to characterise the study population. Categorical variables were expressed as absolute frequencies and proportions; continuous variables as median and interquartile range (IQR). Chi-squared tests were used for comparisons of categorical variables across disease groups, and the Kruskal-Wallis test was used for age comparisons.

Crude mortality rates were calculated as the number of deaths divided by the mid-year population estimate, expressed per 100,000 inhabitants. Age-standardised mortality rates were computed by direct standardisation using the WHO World Standard Population as reference (Ahmad et al. 2001).

Temporal trends were assessed using three complementary approaches:

(i) Poisson regression with a logarithmic link function and population offset was used to estimate the annual percentage change (APC) in mortality rates. Overdispersion was assessed by comparing the deviance to the residual degrees of freedom; when the dispersion ratio exceeded 1.5, negative binomial regression was fitted as a sensitivity analysis (Hilbe 2011).

(ii) Prais-Winsten regression was employed to correct for first-order serial autocorrelation in the time series of mortality rates, classifying trends as increasing, decreasing, or stationary based on the significance of the slope coefficient at α = 0.05 (Antunes & Cardoso 2015).

(iii) Joinpoint analysis was conducted using the segmented regression package in R to identify inflection points in the temporal trend, with the number of joinpoints estimated using the Bayesian Information Criterion (Muggeo 2003, Kim et al. 2000).

Sex-specific mortality rates were calculated using sex-stratified population denominators. The incidence rate ratio (IRR) comparing male-to-female mortality was estimated from the Poisson model with a sex-by-year interaction term.

All analyses were performed in R version 4.5.1 (R Core Team 2025), using the packages tidyverse, gtsummary, MASS, segmented, and patchwork. Statistical significance was set at p < 0.05. Figures were produced following journal standards at 300 dpi resolution.

Ethics — This study was approved by the Research Ethics Committee of the Federal University of Mato Grosso do Sul (CAAE: 74807523.7.0000.0021). As the study used publicly available, de-identified secondary data, informed consent was waived.


RESULTS

Overview and epidemiological profile — During the study period (2013-2022), a total of 76,688 deaths were registered in Campo Grande. Of these, 9,791 (12.8%) had a cardiac disease as the underlying cause of death. One record with indeterminate sex was excluded, yielding 9,791 deaths for analysis.

IHD was the predominant group, accounting for 7,579 deaths (77.4%), followed by HF with 746 (7.6%), ARR with 673 (6.9%), CMP with 514 (5.3%), and INF with 279 (2.9%). Within IHD, acute myocardial infarction (ICD-10: I21) was responsible for 6,468 deaths (85.3% of IHD cases).

The median age at death was 70 years (IQR: 60-80). Age varied significantly across groups (Kruskal-Wallis p < 0.001): ARR had the highest median age (78 years), followed by HF (74), CMP (70), IHD (69), and INF (63). Males accounted for 59.3% of all cardiovascular deaths (n = 5,805). Male predominance was observed in IHD (61.0%), INF (61.6%), and CMP (59.7%), whereas ARR was the only group with female predominance (52.2%). The sex distribution differed significantly across groups (χ² = 65.6, df = 4, p < 0.001).

Regarding race/ethnicity, White individuals comprised 52.9% of deaths, followed by Mixed/Pardo (41.6%), Black (4.1%), Asian (0.9%), and Indigenous (0.4%). The most frequent educational level was primary education (48.8%), followed by secondary (24.2%), illiterate (14.1%), and higher education (7.1%). Data on education were missing for 5.8% of records (Table I).

Place of death — Overall, 48.4% of deaths occurred in hospitals, 32.9% at home, 12.6% in other healthcare facilities, 4.4% in other locations, and 1.6% on public roads. A distinctive pattern emerged for IHD: the proportion of home deaths (38.1%) was remarkably close to hospital deaths (40.0%), a pattern not observed in other groups. For HF, ARR, CMP, and INF, hospital deaths predominated (range: 65.2-92.5%) (Supplementary Fig. S1).

Temporal trends — The overall crude cardiovascular mortality rate increased from 99.3 per 100,000 population in 2013 to 133.6 per 100,000 in 2022, representing a 34.5% increase over the decade. The number of deaths rose from 827 in 2013 to 1,200 in 2022 (+45.1%).

Poisson regression analysis — The overall APC was +3.3% per year (95% CI: 2.5-4.0%; p < 0.001). Given the overdispersion ratio of 3.73, negative binomial regression confirmed a similar APC of +3.2% (Table III). Group-specific trends showed marked heterogeneity:

- IHD: APC +4.3% (95% CI: 3.5-5.1; p < 0.001) — significant increasing trend
- ARR: APC +9.5% (95% CI: 6.6-12.6; p < 0.001) — fastest relative increase
- INF: APC +8.4% (95% CI: 4.0-13.1; p < 0.001) — significant increase
- HF: APC −8.7% (95% CI: −11.0 to −6.4; p < 0.001) — significant declining trend
- CMP: APC −3.8% (95% CI: −6.7 to −0.8; p = 0.012) — significant decline

Prais-Winsten regression — After correcting for serial autocorrelation (ρ = 0.243), the overall trend remained significantly increasing (β = 4.15 per 100,000/year; SE = 1.22; p = 0.011). By group, IHD (β = 3.91; p = 0.001), ARR (β = 0.71; p < 0.001), and INF (β = 0.31; p = 0.001) showed increasing trends, while HF and CMP were classified as stationary after autocorrelation correction (Table III).

Joinpoint analysis — A single joinpoint was identified at 2019 (Fig. 1A). Before 2019, the trend was non-significant (slope: +1.3/100,000 per year; p > 0.05); after 2019, a significant acceleration was observed (slope: +9.0/100,000 per year; p < 0.01), coinciding with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Sex-specific analysis — Males consistently exhibited higher mortality rates than females throughout the study period. The overall IRR was 1.59 (95% CI: 1.47-1.72), indicating that men had 59% higher cardiovascular mortality than women after adjusting for population denominators. Male mortality rose from 127.0 to 163.3 per 100,000, while female mortality rose from 72.9 to 104.6 per 100,000 (Fig. 2B). The sex-by-year interaction was not significant (p = 0.304), indicating that temporal trends were parallel across sexes.

The male-to-female mortality ratio varied by disease group: INF (1.61), IHD (1.56), CMP (1.48), HF (1.06), and ARR (0.92). Thus, ARR was the only group with a female excess in mortality.

Age-specific analysis — The burden of cardiovascular mortality was overwhelmingly concentrated among elderly individuals (≥60 years), who accounted for 75.3% of all deaths. Mortality rates in the ≥60 group ranged from 511 to 729 per 100,000 over the study period, compared with 84-111 per 100,000 in the 40-59 group and 8-18 per 100,000 in the 20-39 group. The age-standardised rates tracked closely with crude rates (Supplementary Fig. S3), confirming that the observed increase was not solely driven by population ageing.

COVID-19 impact — Comparison of the pre-pandemic period (2018-2019) with the pandemic years (2020-2021) revealed a 15.3% increase in the mean cardiovascular mortality rate (from 107.5 to 123.9 per 100,000). This increase was most pronounced for IHD, with 843 deaths in 2020 and 958 in 2021, compared with a pre-pandemic annual average of 765 deaths (2018-2019). The joinpoint at 2019 further supports the pandemic's role in accelerating cardiovascular mortality.


DISCUSSION

This study provides the first comprehensive analysis of cardiovascular mortality trends in Campo Grande over a ten-year period. The main findings were: (i) an increasing overall trend of +3.3% per year, primarily driven by IHD; (ii) heterogeneous trends across disease groups, with ARR and INF increasing and HF and CMP declining; (iii) a 59% higher mortality in males; (iv) a high proportion of IHD deaths occurring at home (38.1%); and (v) a significant acceleration in mortality after 2019, temporally associated with the COVID-19 pandemic.

The increasing trend in IHD mortality contrasts with national data showing declining age-standardised rates between 2000 and 2018 (Lotufo et al. 2021, Mansur & Favarato 2016). However, this national decline has been geographically uneven, with the Central-West region showing slower improvement compared to the South and Southeast (Brant et al. 2025). Municipal-level analyses from the GBD study have revealed that many medium-sized cities outside the southern axis continue to experience rising IHD mortality, particularly when crude rates are considered alongside population ageing (Nascimento et al. 2020). Our findings are consistent with this pattern and underscore the need for intensified cardiovascular prevention strategies in Campo Grande.

The observed APC of +4.3% for IHD is higher than the national average but comparable to rates reported for other Central-West capitals. Silva et al. (2024) documented increasing AMI mortality in several Brazilian regions between 2013 and 2023, particularly in areas with lower healthcare infrastructure density. Santo et al. (2024) similarly identified IHD as a persistent and growing challenge when multiple causes of death are considered, suggesting that official statistics based solely on underlying cause may underestimate the true burden.

The declining trend in HF mortality (−8.7% per year by Poisson regression) aligns with national findings reported by Gaui et al. (2021), who documented a significant decrease in HF mortality between 1980 and 2018, attributed to advances in pharmacological treatment, including the widespread adoption of renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitors and beta-blockers. Stevens et al. (2022) confirmed this declining national trend through 2019. The reclassification of Prais-Winsten results as stationary for HF suggests that the decline may have decelerated in recent years, a hypothesis warranting further investigation.

The marked increase in ARR mortality (+9.5% per year) deserves particular attention. This finding may partly reflect improvements in diagnostic and coding practices for arrhythmia-related deaths, rather than a true epidemiological increase (Oliveira et al. 2024). Nevertheless, the ageing population and increasing prevalence of atrial fibrillation may also contribute to this trend. The female predominance in ARR mortality is consistent with the higher prevalence of atrial fibrillation among elderly women, who constitute a growing demographic segment in Campo Grande.

One of the most clinically significant findings of this study is the high proportion of IHD deaths occurring at home (38.1%), nearly equal to hospital deaths (40.0%). This pattern stands in stark contrast to other disease groups, where hospital deaths predominated (65-93%). Brant et al. (2020a) reported similar findings for AMI in Brazilian state capitals, documenting that 40-50% of AMI deaths occurred out-of-hospital. These deaths likely represent sudden cardiac events in which patients either did not recognise symptoms, delayed calling emergency services, or experienced barriers to accessing timely care. In the context of Campo Grande, this finding has direct implications for public health: it suggests a need for expanded public education campaigns on AMI symptom recognition, strengthened SAMU (Emergency Medical Service) response capacity, and improved distribution of automated external defibrillators in public spaces.

The male predominance in cardiovascular mortality (IRR = 1.59) is consistent with both national and international literature. Santos et al. (2022) documented higher CVD mortality in Brazilian males across all age groups, with sex-specific differences attributed to biological factors (hormonal protection in pre-menopausal women), behavioural risk factors (higher smoking and alcohol consumption among men), and differential healthcare-seeking behaviour. Our finding that the sex gap remained stable over time (non-significant interaction term) suggests that these underlying determinants have not substantially shifted in Campo Grande during the study period.

The racial/ethnic composition of cardiovascular deaths (52.9% White, 41.6% Pardo) broadly reflects the demographic composition of Campo Grande's population. While the proportion of White deaths appears slightly higher than the population share, this may reflect the older age structure of the White population in the city rather than a true excess risk (Lotufo & Bensenor 2024). Educational attainment, with nearly half of deaths occurring among individuals with only primary education, mirrors national data linking lower education to higher cardiovascular mortality (Brant et al. 2021, Cardoso et al. 2021). Nascimento et al. (2022) demonstrated that social vulnerability indices were significantly correlated with cardiovascular mortality at the municipal level in Brazil, reinforcing the role of social determinants.

The acceleration in mortality after 2019, identified by joinpoint analysis, is consistent with the well-documented impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on cardiovascular outcomes. Brant et al. (2020b) demonstrated excess cardiovascular mortality in Brazilian state capitals during 2020, while Normando et al. (2021) reported a 15% reduction in cardiovascular hospitalisations accompanied by increased mortality, suggesting that patients avoided hospitals during the pandemic. Our observed 15.3% increase in cardiovascular mortality during 2020-2021 compared with 2018-2019 is consistent with these findings. Alencar et al. (2022) and Santos et al. (2023) further documented the synergistic effects of COVID-19 on cardiovascular mortality through both direct (myocardial injury, thrombotic events) and indirect (disrupted care pathways) mechanisms. Almeida et al. (2025) analysed cardiovascular mortality subgroups before and during the pandemic nationally, confirming differential impacts across disease categories, consistent with the heterogeneous group-specific trends observed in our study.

The present study has several strengths. It covers a complete decade and uses multiple complementary statistical methods to assess temporal trends. The use of three approaches (Poisson regression, Prais-Winsten regression, and joinpoint analysis) provides robust evidence for trend direction and inflection points. The classification of cardiovascular deaths into pathophysiologically meaningful groups enables differentiated analysis beyond the aggregate CVD category. The data source (SIM) has demonstrated adequate completeness and quality for state capitals in Brazil (Almeida et al. 2025).

Limitations include those inherent to ecological studies using secondary data. Misclassification of the underlying cause of death may affect estimates, particularly for conditions such as arrhythmias and cardiomyopathies where coding practices may vary across institutions and time. The study used population estimates between censuses that carry some uncertainty. Age-standardisation relied on assumed population proportions by age group, which may introduce bias if the true age distribution differs substantially. Furthermore, the study could not assess individual-level risk factors, comorbidities, or treatment histories, limiting causal inferences.

In conclusion, cardiovascular mortality in Campo Grande increased significantly between 2013 and 2022, driven primarily by ischaemic heart disease. The high proportion of out-of-hospital IHD deaths, the increasing trends in arrhythmia-related mortality, and the exacerbation during the COVID-19 pandemic highlight critical gaps in cardiovascular care access and prevention in this Central-West capital. Future research should investigate the spatial distribution of deaths across neighbourhoods, the contribution of risk factor prevalence trends, and the effectiveness of emergency care networks in reducing prehospital cardiovascular mortality.
